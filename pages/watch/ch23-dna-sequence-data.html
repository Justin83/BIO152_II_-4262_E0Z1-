Chapters in This Video

  • Introduction
    Why DNA sequence data and models of evolution are a fascinating, almost philosophical topic.
  • Organizing the data explosion
    The challenge of storing, retrieving, and analyzing sequenced DNA.
  • Two camps: morphology vs. molecules
    Subjective traits vs. repeatable sequence data, plus the fossil record.
  • Homology vs. analogy
    Why bats group with mammals, not birds; parakeets with T. rex, not pterosaurs.
  • Paradigms: nucleotide, RNA, protein
    Different levels of comparison and where information gets lost in translation.
  • Parsimony and the data matrix
    Build the matrix correctly before you build the tree.
  • The pros of molecular data
    Quickly locating trait genes and placing newly discovered organisms.
  • The downsides of DNA data
    Gene location, frozen changes, homoplasy, and only four character states.
  • Pitfalls: hidden changes, convergence, extinct lineages
    Why we need substitution models and weighted data.
  • You can stop here for BIO 152.
    The remaining chapters cover substitution-model math (Jukes–Cantor, Kimura's model, and sequence alignments) and the wrap-up. That material goes beyond what this course covers, so it's optional — feel free to skip it; it won't be on your assessments.
  • Weighting data: the Jukes–Cantor model
    The simplest substitution model, with nucleotides weighted equally.
  • Adding transversions: Kimura's model
    A second parameter and the gamma distribution.
  • Alignments and tree methods
    Distance-based (UPGMA, neighbor joining) vs. character-based methods.
  • Conclusion
    Wrap-up and good luck through the end of the course.
