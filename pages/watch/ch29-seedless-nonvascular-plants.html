Chapters in This Video

  • Seedless, Nonvascular Plants & Mosses
    The lecture places mosses among the four broad land-plant groups and introduces Bryophyta as the working example.
  • Why Mosses Stay Small
    Mosses lack true vascular tissue, so water and nutrients move relatively slowly across short distances.
  • Rhizoids vs. True Roots
    Rhizoids anchor a moss but do not function as true absorptive roots; water uptake occurs across the plant surface.
  • Alternation of Generations: The Core Pattern
    Use the basic sequence as your anchor: gametophyte → gametes → sporophyte → spores → gametophyte.
  • The Moss Gametophyte Is the Visible Plant
    The familiar green moss is the haploid, gametophyte-dominant phase of the life cycle.
  • Male Gametophyte: Antheridia & Sperm
    Male moss gametophytes form antheridia, which produce haploid sperm.
  • Female Gametophyte: Archegonia & Eggs
    Female gametophytes form archegonia, which contain the haploid egg.
  • Water-Dependent Fertilization
    Free water allows motile sperm to travel from antheridia to an egg in an archegonium.
  • Zygote to Dependent Sporophyte
    Fertilization produces a diploid zygote that grows into a sporophyte attached to and dependent on the gametophyte.
  • Sporangium, Meiosis & Spores
    The sporophyte’s sporangium undergoes meiosis to form haploid spores that can begin the next generation.
  • Dependence & Dominance in Mosses
    The sporophyte depends on the gametophyte, while the visible green gametophyte is the dominant stage.
  • Key Takeaways
    Mosses are seedless, remain tied to water for reproduction, and represent an early land-plant strategy.

What to Watch For

This lecture expands Chapter 29: Land Plants through mosses as the working example of seedless, nonvascular plants. Keep the alternation-of-generations framework in view: the haploid gametophyte makes gametes; fertilization produces a diploid zygote; the sporophyte produces haploid spores by meiosis.

Focus on the traits that constrain mosses: they lack true xylem and phloem, remain small, use rhizoids for anchorage rather than absorption, and require free water for sperm to reach an egg. In mosses, the conspicuous green plant is the dominant gametophyte, while the sporophyte remains attached to and nutritionally dependent on it.
