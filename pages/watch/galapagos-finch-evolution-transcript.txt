[00:00] THE MYSTERY OF HOW SPECIES FORM

Our planet has millions of species — over 300,000 beetles alone, 17,000
butterflies, thousands of mammals, fish, and birds, all astonishingly
different. How did so many species come to be? To seek insights into that
question, researchers are focusing on places where species recently arose,
such as the remote Galápagos Islands. Scientists are making observations and
conducting experiments that would have surprised Charles Darwin, and they are
discovering new insights into what the great naturalist called the "mystery
of mysteries": how new species form.

[01:21] THE GALÁPAGOS AND THE GRANTS' FORTY-YEAR STUDY

The Galápagos Islands are one of the most spectacular landscapes in the
world, home to a variety of species that live nowhere else. Biologists Peter
and Rosemary Grant have been seeking answers to how species arise by focusing
on one of the smaller islands, called Daphne Major. "When we started out, we
had no plan for the long term. In fact, we thought it was just going to be a
few years, maybe two years." Two years turned into a 40-year odyssey. The
Grants have returned every summer since 1973. Here they have made some of the
most remarkable observations in the history of field research as they studied
the famed Galápagos finches.

[02:31] THIRTEEN FINCHES: BEAKS AS TOOLS

The finches were first brought to scientists' attention by Charles Darwin,
when his voyage around South America brought him to this cluster of islands
600 miles from mainland Ecuador. These volcanic islands are geologically
young; they began rising from the ocean floor less than 5 million years ago.
At first devoid of life, they now support a modest number of species — among
them, 13 species of finches found in various combinations on the different
islands. The islands differ in size, topography, and height. Larger trees
grow at higher elevations, while low islands have mostly cactus, grasses, and
shrubs. In these diverse habitats, the finches have evolved many ways to
survive. The little warbler finch, with its very fine, needle-like beak, is
perfect for picking off insects. The woodpecker finch has a more robust beak
and concentrates on beetle and termite larvae. The cactus finch has a much
longer, sharp-pointed beak that probes into cactus flowers. And there are the
large, medium, and small ground finches. The basic idea is that the beaks are
tools — you need the right tool for the right job. The finches look so
different that Darwin first mistook them for entirely unrelated kinds of
birds.

[04:54] ONE ANCESTOR: DNA EVIDENCE FOR COMMON DESCENT

How did the Galápagos end up with so many species of finches? Different kinds
of finches could have come from the mainland separately, or they could all
have evolved out on the islands. We now know from DNA evidence that all of
the finches are more closely related to each other than any one is to a
species on the mainland. That tells us only one species arrived on the
archipelago and diversified into the 13 species we see today. They all came
from a single common ancestor. The question then becomes: how did one
ancestral population give rise to many different species, each adapted to a
different lifestyle?

[06:09] MEASURING EVOLUTION: THE 1977 DROUGHT

A crucial insight into how adaptation occurs came when the Grants focused on
one species — the medium ground finch — on the island of Daphne Major. The
small size of the island meant they could walk all over it and follow nearly
every individual. They rose at 5:30 each morning to net the island's medium
ground finches, measuring the size and shape of each bird's beak and its
weight, and tagging them for identification. Year after year they returned,
at times tracking over 1,000 finches. For each bird they knew how many times
it bred, how many mates and offspring it had, and how many of those offspring
survived long enough to breed. Over the first four years, little seemed to
change. Then in 1977 a terrible drought began. Virtually no rain fell for the
next 18 months, and the vegetation practically disappeared. Now the medium
ground finches had to compete for scarce food. They started with small,
medium, and large seeds, but as the small seeds became scarce, they had to
turn increasingly to the large, hard seeds. Only birds with large beaks can
crack open these woody, spiny fruits. The birds with the smallest beaks had
the most trouble. That year, over 80% of the medium ground finches died.

[09:00] NATURAL SELECTION SHIFTS BEAK SIZE

When they inventoried the survivors, they discovered that one trait had made
the greatest difference between life and death. Looking at the distribution
of beak depths in the population, the larger the beak, the higher the
likelihood of surviving the drought of 1977. When they looked at the
offspring, they found an even greater surprise: the average beak depth was
more than 4% larger than in the previous generation. Natural selection had
changed the average beak size. "Could you ever have imagined measuring
something like this on such a short timescale?" "When we started, the answer
is no. We could not imagine we would be able to do it."

[10:03] SELECTION REVERSES: EL NIÑO AND THE 1985 DROUGHT

But was this a fluke, or are changes like this happening all the time? Five
years later, in 1983, an unusually strong El Niño brought ten times more rain
than normal, and the island was overrun by vines that covered even the
cactus. The rains changed the vegetation, so that two years later, when
drought struck again, larger seeds became scarce. Now the birds with larger
beaks had difficulty picking up the more abundant small seeds produced by the
vines. That year, many more finches with small beaks survived, and their
offspring inherited smaller beaks. Selection had swung in the opposite
direction, and evolution had occurred as a result. In an amazingly short
time, the Grants had measured the evolution of beak size not once but twice —
demonstrating that when birds encounter different environments, they change
over a very short amount of time. Over millions of years, changes like these,
occurring throughout the Galápagos, generated all sorts of beak sizes and
shapes.

[11:23] FROM ADAPTATION TO NEW SPECIES: SONG AND MATE CHOICE

But that is only part of the story. How did finches with different beaks
become distinct species? Species are defined as populations whose members do
not interbreed. A typical scenario is that two populations become separated
geographically and undergo enough change in their respective habitats that,
if and when they come into contact again, they do not mate. So the Grants
asked: what keeps different species of finches from mating? On any given
island, the different species sing very different songs. To test whether
songs keep the species apart, the Grants and their student Lorene Ratcliffe
played each species' song through a loudspeaker. When they played the cactus
finch song, the cactus finch came to the loudspeaker and the medium ground
finch ignored it. The males only responded to songs of their own species. The
Grants also looked at whether finches choose mates based on appearance,
putting out stuffed female specimens. The male vigorously courted a female of
his own species and ignored the other, courting only females of similar size
and beak. Song and appearance both play a role in keeping different species
from mating.

[13:38] GEOGRAPHY AND ECOLOGY: HOW ONE SPECIES BECOMES MANY

When populations of the same species are separated, changes in these traits
set the stage for the formation of new species. The Grants have shown that
both geography and ecology are keys to the evolution of the Galápagos
finches. The most likely scenario is that about two million years ago a
single finch population arrived from the mainland. When their descendants
reached another island, they faced new conditions. As those isolated
populations adapted, their traits changed. If the changes included traits
involved in mating, and the populations came into contact again, they no
longer mated — they had become distinct species. While unique to these
remote islands, the history of the Galápagos finches offers a general insight
into why the world is populated with so many species: the more diverse the
environment, the more opportunities for evolutionary change to produce new
species. Over 150 years after Darwin first recognized their significance,
these unassuming birds still illuminate how the great diversity of life arose
— and continues to evolve.
