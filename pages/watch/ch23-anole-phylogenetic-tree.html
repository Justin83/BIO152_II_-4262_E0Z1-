Chapters in This Video

  • So many species: the anole question
    Darwin's quest, and Jonathan Losos's Caribbean lizards.
  • Meet the anoles: dividing up the habitat
    Grass-bush, trunk-ground, twig, and canopy lizards each occupy a different "floor."
  • Are the traits adaptations? Leg length & speed
    Testing running speed: long legs sprint, short legs grip the twig.
  • Climbing leaves: toe pad size
    Why the big canopy lizard hangs on and the ground lizard can't.
  • Watching evolution happen: the island experiment
    Hurricane-cleared islands as labs; legs shorten in just a few generations.
  • From adaptation to speciation: reproductive isolation
    Body-type change isn't the same as making a new species.
  • Dewlaps: the signal that splits species
    How a shift in dewlap color leads to reproductive isolation; micro- vs. macroevolution.
  • Competition and filling the habitats
    New species drive the evolution of different body types.
  • The same body types on every island
    Puerto Rico, Jamaica, Cuba, and Hispaniola share the same forms.
  • Building the phylogenetic tree from DNA
    Aligning sequences and joining species at common-ancestor nodes.
  • The answer: independent (convergent) evolution
    Each island evolved the same body types separately.
  • Why Earth has so many species
    So many habitats, each with many ways to make a living.

What to Watch For

This video ties Chapter 22 and Chapter 23 together with one story. Watch how adaptation and reproductive isolation (dewlap color) build new species, then how sequencing DNA and joining species at common-ancestor nodes produces a phylogenetic tree — the very method from your systematics video. The payoff: the same body types evolved independently on each island (convergent evolution and adaptive radiation).
