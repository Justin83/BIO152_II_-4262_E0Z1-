[00:00] INTRODUCTION

Hello everyone, my name is Justin Howard. I'm presenting via recording since I
can't be there in person. Today I'm talking about the logic of phylogenetics
and how trees are constructed. I hope to cover a good modern definition of
phylogeny versus what was thought before the early-to-mid 19th century, some of
the people whose discoveries brought the field to where it is today (including
the wedge driven between camps when genetic information arrived), how it's used
by researchers today, and — in brief — a few of the ways trees are analyzed and
the options you have.

[00:58] HOW BIG THE TASK IS: GENBANK AND THE SCALE OF LIFE

To show how monumental this is: a 2019 paper I found notes that GenBank — the
genetic-sequence repository hosted on NCBI — had on the order of a few hundred
thousand formally sequenced species, out of an estimated eight or nine million
species on Earth (bacteria, plants, and animals). So there's a long way to go;
we've only scratched the surface.

[01:41] WHAT IS PHYLOGENY? THE MODERN DEFINITION

As a definition, phylogeny is the study of kinds — where you came from. But the
modern definition also takes in that things are related through an ancestral,
evolutionary relationship. Before we knew about genetic information,
classification was based on likeness: this animal has four legs and so does
that one, so I'll group them, then keep classifying by other traits.

[02:20] WHY PHYLOGENY MATTERS

Phylogeny is one of the most pervasive fields. It touches every area of our
lives — understanding how we migrated onto this continent, archaeology
(classifying pottery by the style of its handles), ecology and conservation for
species that may be going extinct, and epidemiology. All of these rely on
discerning evolutionary history.

[03:07] EARLY SYSTEMATISTS AND THEIR LIMITS

Early systematists were limited by what they had to go on. First was what they
could observe with their eyes — and even that depended on how many specimens
had been collected (and who would study them; think of the specimens from
Darwin's Beagle voyage that others had to agree to classify). There was also
the limitation of whether fossils existed for comparison. They used categories
based on observation — whether an organism produced milk, whether it was
torpedo-shaped, embryological development (ontogeny), and so on.

[04:23] LINNAEUS: NAMING BY LIKENESS

The first figure of note in the lead-up to a modern, phylogenetic sense of
classification is Linnaeus. He started a taxonomic system that was more
inclusive at higher ranks (kingdom, domain) and more exclusive as it got more
specific. But this was simply a nomenclature system — naming things and
classifying them by their likeness to other kinds.

[05:06] DARWIN: COMMON ANCESTRY AND THE UNIVERSAL COMMON ANCESTOR

Then came Darwin, with common ancestry, descent with modification, and sexual
selection, which threw a wrench in everything. Even he struggled with what we
should use to classify organisms. In one excerpt he says, in effect, that
similar traits are nearly useless to someone trying to classify, because
likeness says nothing about "blood relationship" — though it matters to the
organism's survival. These ideas led toward answering how things are related in
an evolutionary sense, and ultimately to the idea of a last universal common
ancestor, which flew in the face of much that had been built up to that point.

[06:20] ARISTOTLE & HAECKEL: THE WORD "PHYLOGENY" AND THE LADDER OF LIFE

Even before Linnaeus, Aristotle tried to classify things and had his own ideas.
Haeckel was a bit of a character — originally a German physician with some
deeply troubling ideas about race — but he was important here because he coined
the term "phylogeny" (and other terms we still use), and he was a great artist.
His "tree," though, was really a ladder, with man at the top and everything
placed underneath, not the branching diagram we picture today.

[07:21] MORGAN: THE BIRTH OF GENETICS

Then came Morgan — from my research, roughly the point where genetics was born.
Doing genetic experiments on fruit flies (for which he won the Nobel Prize), he
noticed a single white-eyed male among thousands bred with red eyes and asked
how that came about. He's credited with discovering that chromosomes are
responsible for passing on hereditary traits. He was also a Kentuckian who
attended the University of Kentucky.

[09:09] CRICK & PAULING: CLASSIFY BY DNA, NOT JUST LOOKS

Finally, Francis Crick and Linus Pauling — in separate papers around the same
time — suggested we should start classifying things based on genetic sequencing
rather than just our observations. That was the tipping point. Pauling, one of
my heroes, did enormous work, including explaining sickle-cell anemia; and
Crick and Watson made a huge contribution by illuminating how the DNA molecule
is built.

[09:38] STANDARDIZING THE FIELD: ESSENTIALISM & THE SPECIES DEBATE

Others I haven't named — Fleming, Chargaff, Mendel and his peas, and many more
— all contributed to the point where we had to decide whether to keep
classifying by what we can see or by things hidden from view, like chromosomes
and DNA. Fast-forward to the 1990s (skipping protein sequencing in the '70s and
'80s): a group came together to standardize how we study phylogenetics, based
on a philosophy. Some argue we can't even pin down what a species is, because
things change rapidly and two geographically close species can look and code
very similarly. The more accepted position is that taxonomy should be based on
evolutionary relationship (kinship), not just shared characteristics.
Essentialism — the older idea that everything in nature is already fixed and
we're just here to discover and document it — ran into the theory of evolution,
which holds that things change, sometimes rapidly, and nothing is truly static.
That opened a philosophical debate that continues today.

[11:39] TAXONOMY VS. PHYLOGENY, DEFINED

To clear up the terms: taxonomy is simply a nomenclature system for classifying
things; today it can include morphological characteristics along with DNA, and
it can be supported by phylogeny (and phylogeny by taxonomy). The main
difference is that phylogeny, in the strict sense, concerns DNA and evolutionary
relationships, while taxonomy does not.

[12:15] BUILDING A TREE: THE CARNIVORE SURPRISE (HYENAS GROUP WITH LIONS)

For tree construction I built an example on the website "phyloteam" using 10
characters (the free limit) and a good sampling of organisms — some bacteria, a
"tree octopus," a shark, a bald eagle, a humpback whale, and an order of
carnivores: gray wolf, spotted hyena, and lion. It seems logical to group the
meat-eaters together — but notice the clade (two terminal sister taxa) at the
bottom: the spotted hyena, which you'd think is more canine than feline, is
actually grouped with the lions. That's because of a skull feature — the
chamber for the auditory organs is double-chambered in felines and
single-chambered in canines. This modern tree includes genetic sequencing, and
the branch lengths encode time (molecular clocks and rates of substitution), so
even today we get this peculiar grouping. We can also build trees from the
molecules organisms can produce (like certain amino acids). The more data we
add — billions of sequenced nucleotides — the more statistical testing we need,
which improves our hypotheses.

[14:52] THE FIRST MOLECULAR TREE: WOESE, 1977

Here's the first scientific tree (per my research) based on RNA sequencing, with
statistical testing — Woese and his team, published in 1977. It looks familiar
from undergraduate biology, and if I'm not mistaken, branch "14" was meant for
mammals; it's starting to look more like the phylogenetic trees we use today.

[15:30] PARTS OF A TREE: TAXA, BRANCHES, NODES & ROOTS

Some basic parts: a taxon/species at the tips (a branch can end at species,
genus, or group, depending on how specific you want to be); a branch, which can
correspond to evolutionary time (you can place molecular-clock-derived numbers
above the branches — I left them off because I couldn't fully discern how they
were calculated); a node, where an ancestor gave rise to a new species; and the
root, the last common ancestor. You can choose where to root a tree — at the
base, in the middle of a branch, or rearranged to root from the hyena —
depending on your research needs.

[16:49] MAPPING TRAITS ONTO THE TREE (THE AMNIOTIC EGG)

This example is nice because it includes fossils and living animals. Take a
single trait — say the amniotic egg — and build a tree from it: every "yes"
marks the clade of organisms with an amniotic sac in development. Or integrate
many traits at once (here, six of them) to produce one hypothesis of how the
tree could be constructed. Remember, no single method of inferring relationships
is the standard, but every bit of data we add helps us make a better educated
guess, especially in statistical testing.

[17:46] WHAT MAKES TREE-BUILDING HARD: HOMOPLASY, CLOCKS, FOSSILS

What makes the choice difficult? Homoplasy (described next); the time aspect and
molecular clocks (which rest on theory — even radiometric dating, though
well-founded, is a theory); and the availability of fossils, including whether
we have a whole sequence for an organism or only part.

[18:13] HOMOLOGY VS. HOMOPLASY & CONVERGENT EVOLUTION (THE OCTOPUS EYE)

Homology means species share a common ancestor: if species A, B, and C share a
trait (say, a stinger), we infer one common ancestor for them, while D evolved
its version separately. Homoplasy is a confounding factor: if A and B share a
placenta, it could have evolved two separate times — convergent evolution,
where similar traits develop in distantly related species. A great example is
the octopus eye, which is strikingly similar to the human eye (a lens that
focuses light onto a retina, with neurons forming an image) even though
mammals and octopuses are far removed on the tree. Those similarities — plus
the octopus's problem-solving and rapid camouflage — make it a fascinating
model, and a clear case of why convergent evolution can be studied with
phylogenetic trees.

[20:36] ANALYZING TREES: PARSIMONY

Now, some ways of analyzing trees. Parsimony is my favorite so far and is the
simplest by definition. If species A and B share a placenta, the simplest
explanation is that they descend from an ancestor that had one. Taking the
earlier character matrix, we can form two hypotheses: one requires six
evolutionary events, the other seven. Parsimony favors the first — the simplest
— because it requires the least guessing. The goal is a tree that keeps you from
having to add hypothesis after hypothesis to explain the branching: it's simpler
to say a ray-finned fish and the others are closely related than to claim a bony
skeleton arose independently on two separate occasions.

[22:28] LIKELIHOOD (OPTIONAL)

Likelihood isn't much harder, and I like the mode of thinking behind it.
Depending on the parameters we choose — say, the genetic-sequence relatedness
between two organisms — we can take the rate of substitution (or the frequency
of a nucleotide's appearance), put it into a computer, and ask it to arrange the
data in many ways (for example, 10,000 permutations). Statistical modeling gives
a bell curve, and we pick the most likely arrangement in the middle for our
tree. We can add other parameters, like branch length, and test hypotheses about
them. The number of options grows exponentially fast as taxa increase — so
besides genetic information, computers were a huge help to phylogenetics.

[24:17] BAYESIAN METHODS & WRAP-UP (OPTIONAL)

The Bayesian method also does theoretical, statistical testing, but instead of
saying "this is the most common arrangement we found," it assigns a probability
that a given arrangement is true — much like saying "there's a 2% chance of rain
tomorrow," we might say "there's a 2% chance these taxa are related this way."
Hopefully I've been clear about the logic of phylogenetic trees — it really was
an exercise in logic and philosophy as much as biology. I hope you enjoyed it,
and I wish you all the best of luck and a great rest of your week. Thank you.
