Chapters in This Video

  • True Vascular Tissue & Larger Plants
    Ferns illustrate how true vascular tissue supports efficient transport and allows larger plant bodies.
  • Xylem: Water from Roots to Shoots
    The lecture introduces xylem as the tissue that carries water upward from roots into the shoot system.
  • Phloem: Transporting Sugars
    Phloem distributes photosynthetic products from source tissues to metabolically active or storage sinks.
  • Roots, Rhizoids & Ferns
    True roots absorb water and nutrients, whereas rhizoids mainly anchor nonvascular plants; ferns are the representative seedless vascular group.
  • Carboniferous Forests, Coal & Climate
    Dense ancient fern forests helped sequester carbon, creating coal deposits and linking plant history to modern climate change.
  • Alternation of Generations in Ferns
    The same broad life-cycle pattern applies to ferns, but the dominant generation differs from mosses.
  • The Fern Is the Diploid Sporophyte
    The large fern plant is the diploid sporophyte; sporangia on fronds make haploid spores by meiosis.
  • Spores Grow into a Prothallus
    A germinating fern spore develops into the small, haploid gametophyte called a prothallus.
  • Antheridia & Archegonia on the Prothallus
    The fern gametophyte contains both male and female reproductive structures, producing sperm and eggs.
  • Water, Fertilization & Genetic Variation
    Sperm require free water to reach an egg; timing of reproductive maturity can reduce self-fertilization.
  • New Sporophyte Formation
    Fertilization creates a diploid zygote that develops into a new, independent fern sporophyte.
  • Fern Life-Cycle Summary
    Ferns are seedless and still water-dependent for fertilization, but their sporophytes become nutritionally independent.
  • Vocabulary & Transition to Seed Plants
    Review prothallus, antheridia, archegonia, sporangium, meiosis, and spores before moving to seed plants.

What to Watch For

This lecture continues Chapter 29: Land Plants with ferns as the model seedless vascular plants. Compare ferns directly with mosses: true xylem and phloem support efficient transport and larger body size; true roots absorb water and nutrients; and the visible fern is the dominant, independent diploid sporophyte.

Follow the fern life cycle carefully: sporangia make haploid spores by meiosis; spores grow into the small haploid prothallus (gametophyte); antheridia make sperm and archegonia contain eggs; water still allows sperm to reach an egg; fertilization begins a new sporophyte. Phloem transports sugars from source tissues to metabolic or storage sinks, so its direction is determined by source–sink relationships rather than always being “down.”
