Chapters in This Video

  • What are protists?
    Pond water diversity: flagella, cilia, amoebas, and cells that just sit in the light. All share a nucleus and organelles — single-cell eukaryotes are called protists.
  • The classification challenge
    Some protist lineages diverged long before animals and fungi did. The old single-kingdom idea needs revision; biologists now use genetic data to untangle their relationships.
  • Euglenids
    Euglena navigates with a flagellum and a light-sensitive eye spot. Not all euglenids are green — Astasia lost its chloroplasts; Paranema uses its flagellum as a whip to capture prey.
  • Diatoms and their relatives
    Diatoms build finely sculpted glass cases from dissolved silica. Their evolutionary branch also includes golden colonial cells and fungi-like water molds — looks can be deceiving; DNA tells the true story.
  • Amoebas
    Amoeba locomotion has fascinated biologists since Leeuwenhoek. Arcella secretes a shell; Diflugia cements mineral fragments into one. Heliozoans use amoeba-like pseudopodia to engulf prey.
  • Green algae
    Clostarium, filamentous Spirogyra (conjugation), and colonial forms from Gonium to Volvox. Colonial protists like Volvox let us imagine how multicellular plants evolved from single cells.
  • Protists inside animals: parasites and symbionts
    Plasmodium causes malaria; Trypanosoma causes sleeping sickness. But not all insiders are harmful — Trichonympha in termite guts breaks down wood in a mutualistic partnership.
  • Ciliates: the dinosaurs of microspace
    Paramecium, Lacrymaria, Euplotes, Vorticella, Stentor, Didinium, suctorians, and more. Cilia structure is animated; Didinium's hunt of paramecium is one of the most dramatic predator-prey interactions in the micro world.
  • What protists tell us
    Observing protists is a window into early eukaryotic life. Their advances in cell metabolism, genetic control, and behavior set the stage for all multicellular organisms.

What to Watch For

This video is your visual anchor for Chapter 28: Protists. As you watch, connect what you see to the chapter vocabulary: the difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, diverse modes of nutrition (photosynthesis, heterotrophy, absorption), and the key idea that protists are not a natural group — they are defined by what they are not (not bacteria, not fungi, not animals, not plants).

Pay close attention to how each group moves and feeds: euglenid phototaxis via the eye spot, diatom silica shells, amoeba pseudopodia, Spirogyra conjugation, and ciliate structure (the 9+2 microtubule arrangement). The parasites section (Plasmodium and Trypanosoma) connects directly to human health content in the chapter. The termite-gut section is a clean example of mutualism at the cellular level.
