Chapters in This Video

  • Introduction
    What this talk covers: the logic of phylogenetics and how trees are built.
  • How big the task is: GenBank & the scale of life
    A few hundred thousand sequenced species out of millions on Earth.
  • What is phylogeny? The modern definition
    Ancestral, evolutionary relationship vs. classifying by likeness.
  • Why phylogeny matters
    Migration, archaeology, ecology & conservation, epidemiology.
  • Early systematists and their limits
    Observation, available specimens, and whether fossils existed.
  • Linnaeus: naming by likeness
    A nomenclature system, more inclusive at higher ranks.
  • Darwin: common ancestry & the universal common ancestor
    Why likeness says nothing about “blood relationship.”
  • Aristotle & Haeckel: the word “phylogeny” and the ladder of life
    Haeckel coined the term but drew a ladder, not a branching tree.
  • Morgan: the birth of genetics
    Fruit flies, white eyes, and chromosomes carrying heredity.
  • Crick & Pauling: classify by DNA, not just looks
    The tipping point toward genetic-sequence classification.
  • Standardizing the field: essentialism & the species debate
    Kinship over shared traits; a changing nature vs. a fixed one.
  • Taxonomy vs. phylogeny, defined
    One concerns DNA and evolutionary relationships; the other does not.
  • Building a tree: the carnivore surprise
    Why the spotted hyena groups with lions, not wolves (skull anatomy).
  • The first molecular tree: Woese, 1977
    A tree built from RNA sequencing with statistical testing.
  • Parts of a tree: taxa, branches, nodes & roots
    What each element means and how rooting changes the picture.
  • Mapping traits onto the tree (the amniotic egg)
    Building from one trait, then integrating many into one hypothesis.
  • What makes tree-building hard: homoplasy, clocks, fossils
    The confounders behind every tree.
  • Homology vs. homoplasy & convergent evolution
    Shared ancestry vs. the octopus eye evolving independently.
  • Analyzing trees: parsimony
    The simplest explanation — fewest evolutionary events — wins.
  • You can stop here for BIO 152 (about the 22:30 mark).
    The remaining sections cover the Likelihood and Bayesian tree-analysis methods plus the wrap-up. That material is a supplemental deep-dive beyond what this course covers, so it's optional — feel free to skip it; it won't be on your assessments.
  • Likelihood · optional
    Permutations, bell curves, and picking the most likely tree.
  • Bayesian methods & wrap-up · optional
    Assigning a probability that a tree is true, and closing thoughts.
