[0:12] PLANTS, HUMAN HEALTH, AND THE SCALE OF THE FOOD CHALLENGE

Human health and well-being — as well as environmental health and sustainability — are dependent on the health of plant systems. Eleven plant species provide over 90% of the calories that make up the human diet. Over the next 30 years, the world's population will increase from approximately 7 billion to over 9 billion people. To feed the additional 2 billion, we will need to grow roughly 70% more food than we currently produce.

We will also need to protect that food from loss. Up to 30% of the food produced globally is lost to diseases, insects, and spoilage before it is ever consumed. To feed a larger world, we must both produce more and lose less.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[1:03] WHEAT: THE WORLD'S MOST WIDELY PLANTED CROP

Wheat is the second most important food crop globally, providing approximately 17% of the calories that sustain humans. Wheat is planted on more acreage around the world than any other food crop, making it critical to global food security.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[1:19] WHAT IS WHEAT BLAST?

Wheat blast is an emerging disease of wheat in South America caused by a fungus called Magnaporthe oryzae triticum pathotype. It was first discovered in Brazil in 1985 and has since spread to Bolivia and Paraguay. In affected fields, yields can be reduced by 50 to 90%. Diseased wheat plants either fail to produce grain entirely or produce grain of very poor quality. During the wheat blast epidemic in Brazil in 2009, national wheat production fell by 30%.

Unlike the rust diseases that U.S. wheat producers are accustomed to managing, fungicides have not been effective against wheat blast in Brazil or Bolivia. Without a better understanding of the pathogen and the disease cycle, selecting and applying effective fungicide chemistry remains difficult.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[2:21] U.S. RESEARCH RESPONSE: PROJECT ONE (2008)

In 2008, the United States Department of Agriculture funded a team of scientists at Kansas State University and the USDA Agricultural Research Service at Fort Detrick, Maryland, to investigate wheat blast and determine the risk it poses to the U.S. wheat industry. The research team set out to accomplish four goals:

1. Determine which U.S. wheat varieties are resistant to blast and which are susceptible.
2. Develop molecular tools for detection of the pathogen and diagnosis of the disease.
3. Increase scientific understanding of the biology of the pathogen.
4. Educate stakeholders and scientists about this potential threat to U.S. wheat production.

Because wheat blast symptoms in South America are often confused with other wheat diseases, accurate molecular-based detection and diagnostic tools are essential to effective disease management.

Working in specially designed biocontainment laboratories at Kansas State University and at Fort Detrick, researchers tested over 500 U.S. commercial wheat varieties for susceptibility. Wheat varieties with varying levels of resistance were identified. Through collaborations with scientists in Bolivia and Brazil, resistance findings determined in U.S. laboratories were verified in South America under natural field conditions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[4:14] PROJECT TWO: EXPANDED SCOPE

A second USDA project was later established to build on the first. Joining Kansas State University and the Agricultural Research Service are scientists from several additional U.S. and Brazilian institutions. The new project includes three additional goals:

1. Development of a wheat blast forecasting system.
2. Improved fungicide management strategies.
3. A better understanding of the wheat blast disease cycle.

Together, these projects are designed to provide the mitigation measures necessary to manage wheat blast and to minimize its impacts as an emerging disease.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[4:56] CONCLUSION: PROTECTING THE PLANTS WE DEPEND ON

To meet the challenge of feeding 9 billion people, we need to produce more food and protect that food from diseases that reduce yield and lower quality. Wheat supplies approximately 17% of the calories humans consume. If we are to feed a growing population, we must do the science and provide the education necessary to ensure an adequate and dependable supply of wheat-based foods.
