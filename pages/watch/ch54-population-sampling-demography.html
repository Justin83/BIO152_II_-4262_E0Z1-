Important Clarifications

Mark–recapture assumptions: Estimates are most reliable when marks remain visible, marked organisms mix back into the population, capture probabilities are similar, and births, deaths, immigration, and emigration are limited during the study.

Birth rate: Natality is usually expressed as births per unit population per unit time, not simply the number of individuals that give birth.

Population pyramids: A broad base suggests many pre-reproductive individuals and likely future growth. A more column-shaped population suggests relative stability. A narrow base suggests slower growth or possible decline. These are predictions, not guarantees.

Type III survivorship: High juvenile mortality does not automatically mean that a species provides no parental care.

What to Watch For

Compare direct counts, area sampling, indirect signs, camera observations, and mark–recapture. Each method produces an estimate with assumptions and possible sources of error.

Distinguish population density from dispersion. Then identify random, clumped, and uniform patterns and explain what biological or environmental processes could create them.

Use the human population pyramid to connect age structure with future population growth. A large pre-reproductive group can create population momentum even if birth rates later decline.

Interpret survivorship curves by asking whether mortality is concentrated late in life, remains approximately constant, or is concentrated early in life.
