[00:00] SO MANY SPECIES: THE ANOLE QUESTION

The diversity of animals on our planet is breathtaking — millions of species
adapted to all kinds of habitats. Ever since Darwin, understanding how so many
species evolved has been a major quest of biology, and of biologists like
Jonathan Losos. In the Caribbean, he's studying a remarkable group of lizards,
finding clues to their origins in their bodies, their lifestyles, and in their
DNA. These lizards are providing fresh insight into both how new species form
and why our world is filled with so many creatures.

[01:19] MEET THE ANOLES: DIVIDING UP THE HABITAT

Here in Puerto Rico, Jonathan is stalking lizards called anoles; with almost
30 years of practice, he's a pro at catching them. Puerto Rico's anoles all
feed on similar food — mostly small prey like spiders and crickets — but they
divide up their habitats in a clever way. The long-tailed, slender grass-bush
anole lives in grasses and bushes. On the low parts of tree trunks and on the
ground, a longer-legged, stockier trunk-ground anole forages. On twigs and
small branches we find very small, short-legged twig anoles. And high up in the
canopy lives a large green lizard with big toe pads. Like apartment dwellers,
each species lives in a different vertical space — and each "floor" offers
unique evolutionary opportunities to its inhabitants.

[03:23] ARE THE TRAITS ADAPTATIONS? LEG LENGTH AND SPEED

The fact that lizards differ in leg length and toe-pad size depending on where
they live suggests these traits are adaptations to their habitats. To test
that, the team compared the running ability of two lizards — one with long
legs, one with short. The ground lizard is a sprinter: she lives at the bottom
of trees in the open and has to run down quickly to catch prey. The
shorter-legged twig lizard is not nearly as fast, which seems like a
disadvantage. So why aren't its legs longer? On a thin branch, the twig lizard
scurries along comfortably like a balance beam; instead of speed, its legs
provide a firm grasp. Put the long-legged sprinter on the same narrow stick and
she's ungainly — her legs are too long. On twigs, long legs only increase the
chance of falling. So ground lizards evolved long legs and twig lizards short
ones, each enabling their lifestyle.

[05:34] CLIMBING LEAVES: TOE PAD SIZE

Next, the team compared how well two species climb the slick surfaces of
leaves. Anoles have different-sized toe pads on their feet. The ground lizard
can barely hang on. But the big canopy lizard — despite his weight — climbs
easily, using microscopic hairs on his toe pads to bond with the surface. Even
for his size, he has particularly large toe pads. That's an adaptation, because
he cannot afford to fall out of the canopy.

[06:54] WATCHING EVOLUTION HAPPEN: THE ISLAND EXPERIMENT

But how do these adaptations arise? Jonathan and his colleagues wanted to see
if they could actually observe the lizards' traits evolve. Their inspiration
was the rapidly changing environment of some of the smallest Caribbean islands,
where hurricanes occasionally swamp the land and scrub it free of lizards. The
team realized they could use these depleted islands as laboratories. They
captured tree-dwelling anoles on a larger island, then visited seven
hurricane-cleared islands and placed a female and male anole on each. These
islands have no trees, only small bushes — so how would long-legged lizards
fare on thin branches? The next year, the scientists returned and found the
mating pairs had not only survived but reproduced, and the new populations had
taken to living on thin branches. Each time they found a lizard, they measured
how high it was off the ground, the diameter of its perch, and its posture;
back at the field lab, they x-rayed the lizards to measure leg length precisely
and scanned their toe pads, then returned each animal to the exact spot. A year
later they came back and discovered the average lizard leg had shortened in
just two generations. Over four years, the populations got shorter and shorter
legs. Evolution can occur very rapidly when natural selection is strong.

[10:05] FROM ADAPTATION TO SPECIATION: REPRODUCTIVE ISOLATION

Adaptations like these explain how different body types evolve, but they do not
explain how new anole species arise. It's changes in other traits that play a
key role in speciation. Two groups of animals are defined as different species
when individuals from one group don't mate and reproduce with those from the
other. So for a population to become a new species, something has to prevent
its members from breeding with closely related populations — this is called
reproductive isolation. One way a species can split in two is for populations
to separate geographically; over many generations they undergo enough change in
their respective habitats that, if and when they come back together, they don't
mate.

[11:01] DEWLAPS: THE SIGNAL THAT SPLITS SPECIES

So what kind of changes keep anoles from mating? Anoles have a flap of skin
under their throats called a dewlap, which males display to attract females —
and remarkably, every species in the same area has a different dewlap. So a
change in a dewlap is a critical step in forming new anole species. Why would
dewlap colors change? Consider a grass lizard living in a relatively dark
forest: its dewlap is light-colored. Now suppose a population ends up in a much
more open, sunnier area, where a light dewlap isn't very effective. Over time
the population would evolve by natural selection to have a darker dewlap, much
more visible in open habitat. If those two populations later come together, the
females would no longer recognize the males as the same species; they wouldn't
mate — they'd be reproductively isolated. There's a simple connection between
changes within populations (microevolution) and the formation of new species
(macroevolution): when changes within populations include traits involved in
mating, like dewlap color, the stage is set for new species.

[12:41] COMPETITION AND FILLING THE HABITATS

Once new species have formed, competition drives the evolution of different
body types. Species living in the same area compete for resources. But if
members of one species move into another habitat, they can use resources
unavailable to the others. Over many generations, natural selection favors
traits that let species occupy different habitats — the process that produced
the body types we see in Puerto Rico.

[13:35] THE SAME BODY TYPES ON EVERY ISLAND

And not just there. On each of the Caribbean's four largest islands — Puerto
Rico, Jamaica, Cuba, and Hispaniola — we find the same distribution of
similar-looking lizards. You'd think the lizards on different islands would look
different, but each island has the same basic body types: slender, long-tailed
grass-bush anoles; long-legged trunk-ground anoles; short-legged twig anoles;
and canopy anoles with large toe pads.

[14:30] BUILDING THE PHYLOGENETIC TREE FROM DNA

How did each island end up with the same body types? Did each type evolve once
and then spread to the other islands, or did each type evolve independently on
each island? To find out, Jonathan and his colleagues sequenced the DNA of
anoles from each island, examining the same stretch of DNA from many species to
uncover their evolutionary relationships. Species that are closely related have
few differences in their DNA — two species might differ at only one base pair —
while a species that diverged long ago has many differences. After determining
which two species were most closely related, the team joined them with a node
representing a common ancestor, then joined those to the next most closely
related, and so on, until all the lizards were united in a phylogenetic tree.

[15:31] THE ANSWER: INDEPENDENT (CONVERGENT) EVOLUTION

The DNA revealed that lizards on each island tend to be more closely related to
one another than to similar-looking lizards on other islands. That means the
same types of lizards generally evolved independently on each island. On all
the large Caribbean islands, the same traits — body color, limb length, toe-pad
size — have evolved again and again.

[16:16] WHY EARTH HAS SO MANY SPECIES

This repeated filling of habitats on each island illustrates why our planet has
so many species: there are so many habitats, and each provides numerous ways to
survive. In the Serengeti, zebras eat the tallest, coarsest grass, wildebeest
the medium-height grass, and Thompson's gazelles the shortest. In the
Galápagos, some finches primarily eat seeds on the ground and others insects in
the trees. Look around you — in your backyard or around the world — and you'll
find so many different environments, each full of creatures making a living in a
different way.
