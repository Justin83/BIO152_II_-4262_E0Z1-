Meet Robert Sapolsky

Robert Sapolsky is a Stanford professor whose work crosses biology, neuroscience, neurology, and neurosurgery. He is also a field primatologist who spent years studying wild baboons in East Africa.

What makes him especially interesting is the way he moves comfortably from animal behavior and evolution to hormones, stress physiology, brain circuits, development, and human behavior. His lectures are funny, wide-ranging, and unusually good at showing why a complicated biological question rarely has only one correct level of explanation. You can read more on his Stanford profile.

My Recommendation

I recommend this short excerpt because it deepens the chapter’s distinction between proximate and ultimate explanations. Watch 20:33–25:17.

If this style of biological thinking appeals to you, consider returning to the full lecture or exploring more of Sapolsky’s videos in the future. His Human Behavioral Biology material is longer and more advanced than BIO 152 requires, and some lectures use mature human-behavior examples, but they are excellent enrichment for students interested in behavior, neuroscience, stress, or human biology.

Chapters in This Excerpt

  • The chicken-crossing-the-road question
    Introduces one behavior that can be explained from several biological perspectives.
  • Hormones, anatomy, and evolution
    Shows how endocrinology, anatomy, and evolutionary biology can each provide a valid part of the explanation.
  • The danger of explanatory “buckets”
    Warns against treating a gene, neurotransmitter, hormone, or childhood experience as the one complete cause.
  • What happened immediately before the behavior?
    Begins with neurons and circuitry acting in the fraction of a second before an action.
  • Working backward through biology
    Adds sensory cues, hormones, development, genes, population variation, and evolutionary history.
  • Why the levels cannot be separated
    Explains that hormones connect to genes, genes connect to selection, and present responses connect to development.
  • Temporary platforms, not separate causes
    Concludes that biological levels are convenient viewpoints on one continuous causal history.

What to Watch For

Notice that hormones, neurons, sensory stimuli, anatomy, development, genes, population history, and evolution are not competing answers. They are connected parts of the same explanation.

Ask how the excerpt extends the distinction between proximate causes and ultimate causes. Sapolsky is effectively unpacking several mechanisms, developmental influences, and evolutionary histories that converge on one behavior.

Think about it: Why is saying “the behavior is caused by hormones” incomplete? Identify at least two additional biological levels that could contribute.
