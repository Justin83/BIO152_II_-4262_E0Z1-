Chapters in This Video

  • Bioluminescent Bays: The Phenomenon
    Coastal waters in a few special places light up at night with countless tiny blue flashes — and it all comes down to science.
  • What Are Dinoflagellates?
    Single-celled protists — eukaryotes with a nucleus but outside the plant, animal, and fungal kingdoms.
  • Size, Habitat & Feeding
    Microscopic to 2 mm (sea sparkle); marine and freshwater; photosynthetic or heterotrophic species.
  • How Many Species Bioluminesce?
    Only about 70 of the hundreds of dinoflagellate species produce light.
  • Why Glow? Defense Mechanisms
    Bioluminescence costs energy, so there must be a survival benefit — startling copepod predators or attracting larger ones.
  • The Biochemistry: Luciferase & Luciferin
    Motion stretches the cell membrane, activating scintillons that lower pH and trigger the luciferase–luciferin light-producing reaction.
  • Why Blue Light?
    Blue light penetrates deepest in water since water absorbs other wavelengths more quickly — an evolutionary advantage.
  • Toxins & Red Tides
    Toxic species produce saxitoxin; red tides and their ecological and human-health impacts.
  • Bioluminescent Bays: Ecology & Threats
    A handful of tropical bays glow consistently; fragile ecosystems threatened by pesticides, storms, and disrupted nutrient cycles.

What to Watch For

This 5-minute video zooms into one of the most striking behaviors covered in Chapter 28: Protists — bioluminescence in dinoflagellates. As you watch, pay attention to how dinoflagellates illustrate core protist features: eukaryotic cell structure, ecological diversity (photosynthetic vs. heterotrophic), and key roles in marine food webs.

Focus on the chemistry of light production: the enzyme luciferase, the substrate luciferin, and the cellular structures called scintillons. Also note the ecological double-edge: dinoflagellates as food source and toxin producers. Saxitoxin and red tides connect Chapter 28 protist biology to real-world human-health and ecosystem impacts.
