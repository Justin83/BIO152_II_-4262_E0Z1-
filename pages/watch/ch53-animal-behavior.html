Optional Deeper Look: One Behavior, Many Biological Explanations

Watch 20:33–25:17 only. Robert Sapolsky uses a chicken-crossing-the-road example to show that a single behavior can be studied at several connected biological levels: immediate sensory cues, neural circuits, hormones, anatomy, development, genes, population differences, and evolution.

This expands the chapter’s proximate-versus-ultimate framework. A hormone or gene may contribute to a behavior, but neither is automatically the complete explanation.

Corrections and Clarifications

Innate and learned behavior: These are useful endpoints on a continuum. Most behaviors develop through interactions among genes, anatomy, physiology, experience, and environmental conditions.

Fixed action patterns: Introductory descriptions often present these responses as completely rigid. In practice, behavioral responses can vary with context, development, and the condition of the animal.

Imprinting: Imprinting is a specialized form of learning that occurs during a sensitive period. It is more constrained than simply following the first living organism encountered.

What to Watch For

Be able to contrast proximate explanations, which address mechanisms and development, with ultimate explanations, which address evolutionary history and adaptive significance.

Use the pumpkin toadlet example to connect anatomy and physical constraints with behavior. The poor jump is not itself advantageous; it is a consequence of extreme miniaturization, which may provide other benefits.

Organize reflexes, fixed action patterns, biological rhythms, imprinting, spatial learning, cognition, and problem-solving along an innate-to-learned continuum.

Pay special attention to the honeybee waggle-dance experiment. It shows that an inherited capacity can still be refined through social experience.

The Sapolsky excerpt reinforces that neural activity, hormones, development, genes, and evolution are complementary levels of explanation rather than competing single causes.

Chapter 53 also develops behavioral genetics, navigation, reproductive strategies, altruism, kin selection, and group living.
