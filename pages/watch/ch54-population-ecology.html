The later life-history section is not assigned because the main video already introduces that topic.

Population pyramids: A broad base indicates many pre-reproductive individuals and suggests continued growth; a more column-shaped distribution suggests relative stability; a narrow base suggests slower growth or possible decline.

Mark–recapture: Estimates are most reliable when marks remain visible, marked individuals mix back into the population, capture probability is similar among individuals, and births, deaths, immigration, and emigration are limited during the study.

Type III survivorship: High juvenile mortality does not automatically mean that a species provides no parental care.

Population Connections Not Fully Developed in the Main Video

Population versus metapopulation: A population consists of members of one species living in the same area. A metapopulation is a set of separated local populations connected by dispersal. A local population may disappear from one habitat patch while immigrants from another patch later recolonize it. Habitat fragmentation and wildlife corridors therefore affect the persistence of the entire metapopulation.

Allee effect: High density can limit population growth, but extremely low density can also reduce survival or reproduction. Individuals may have difficulty finding mates, cooperating in feeding or defense, pollinating one another, or maintaining genetic diversity. This is especially important in conservation biology.

Corrections and Clarifications

Population change: Births and immigration add individuals; deaths and emigration remove individuals. This describes observed population change. The intrinsic rate of increase is a more specific per-capita growth concept and is not simply the four-term population-balance equation.

Limiting resources and factors: Food, water, shelter, nutrients, and space are resources. Disease can be a density-dependent limiting factor, but it is not a resource.

Density-independent regulation: Density-independent does not mean that an event is purely random or that every individual is affected equally. It means the per-capita effect is not determined by population density.

Carrying capacity: Carrying capacity is not permanently fixed. It can change when resources, habitat quality, climate, competitors, predators, or disease conditions change.

Life-history strategies: The traditional r-selected and K-selected categories are simplified endpoints. Real species show combinations of traits along multiple life-history continua.

Hare–lynx example: The classic Hudson Bay Company cycle involves the snowshoe hare and Canada lynx, not the Arctic hare.

Final recap: Population size is decreased by emigration and deaths. The video’s closing use of “immigration” in that sentence is a verbal slip.

What to Watch For

Know the four processes that change population size: births, deaths, immigration, and emigration. Also distinguish size from density, dispersion, sex ratio, and age structure.

Compare the assumptions and shapes of exponential growth and logistic growth. Exponential growth produces a J-shaped curve; logistic growth incorporates carrying capacity and produces an S-shaped curve.

Distinguish density-dependent factors such as competition, disease, and predation from density-independent factors such as some fires, floods, freezes, and storms.

Use the hare–lynx example to explain how predator and prey populations can cycle with a time lag.

Use age-structure diagrams to predict whether a human population is likely to grow, remain relatively stable, or decline.

Distinguish a local population from a metapopulation and explain how dispersal can allow recolonization after local extinction.
