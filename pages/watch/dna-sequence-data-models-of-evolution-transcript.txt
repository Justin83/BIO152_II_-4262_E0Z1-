[00:00] INTRODUCTION

Good morning, everyone. My name is Justin Howard, and this is my second
presentation from biosystematics, on DNA sequence data and DNA models of
evolution. I will keep this concise — I have heavily edited out the ums and
pauses to make it more brief and tolerable. This is a very interesting
subject; I do not think I have studied anything more in my last two years at
Western. It is almost more philosophical than biological, with a lot of
complicated statistical theory and ideas about how we should count things in
this field. It has been very interesting, very challenging, and I really
enjoyed it.

[00:48] ORGANIZING THE DATA EXPLOSION

What I want to talk about today is, first, the challenges systematists faced
before DNA information was available, and then the increased challenge of
organizing this data as it became available. There has been an explosion as
everyone started to sequence DNA and deposit it in different databanks. That
posed challenges in how we analyze it, how we retrieve it, and how we
interpret it within the context of a model of evolution.

[01:23] TWO CAMPS: MORPHOLOGY VS. MOLECULAR DATA

It seems like everybody always starts with a Darwin quote. This one caught my
eye because Darwin seemed to be lamenting that he knew about evolutionary
change and ancestry, but he was never going to actually see it happen — he
could only observe life as it is at one moment in time. When he published his
theory and people got on board, it caused real issues in how we classify
things. Ultimately everyone split into two camps. Some argued that basing
classification on morphological characteristics is subjective — based on
opinion — whereas new DNA sequence information is more independent, objective,
and repeatable, and so it should be the basis. We also had to decide what to
do with the fossil record, which was a big part of updating relationships,
divergences, and the rise of new species. I believe the consensus today is to
use all of that information and combine it. A chapter by Brent Mishler that
Dr. Phillips provided really put all of this into context for me.

[03:05] HOMOLOGY VS. ANALOGY

If we are going to do deep phylogenetic studies, and also look at recent or
fast-changing relationships — when discerning a new medicine, or where a
disease or a bad phenotype is coming from — we need to use all of it. One
challenge Darwin faced: he might have grouped two-winged animals like bats
together with birds. But molecular data shows that bats, which are mammals,
are more closely related to gorillas than to birds. Likewise, I would not put
alligators more closely related to eagles than to monitor lizards. The DNA
sequence data does not lie; it is one of the ways we are able to reorganize
the trees we use to discern relationships among organisms. So, to get a
couple of terms down: one challenge faced before molecular data was the
problem of homology. The bat wing and the eagle wing might be grouped
together because they are observable and look alike, but they are not actually
homologous traits. Today we can support or refute such groupings based on a
phylogenetic analysis, using both fossil and morphological data. As another
example, you would obviously group two winged animals together, but in fact
parakeets (birds) are more related to T. rex than to the pterosaurs.

[04:33] PARADIGMS: NUCLEOTIDE, RNA, AND PROTEIN

It was easy for me to get sidetracked deciding which paradigm to look at the
sequence data from. By paradigm, I mean I can look at the nucleotide
sequences and how they are directly related or homologous to another
organism, or I can look at the RNA encoded by them, or the functional
proteins that have similar jobs between different groups of organisms. At each
level I can infer relationships, and some are more useful than others
depending on context. It gets a little crazy with amino acids, because more
than one codon can code for the same amino acid, and amino acids can be
swapped while a protein keeps a similar function — so information can get lost
in translation, and classification gets tricky.

[05:30] PARSIMONY AND THE DATA MATRIX

When viewing all this data from different angles — codon, protein, or DNA
sequence — it is important to remember the principle of parsimony. In an ideal
situation, as Mishler stated, any good systematist would put their data into a
matrix first, before building a tree. Technically, you would want that tree to
be reversible back to a matrix with the same data present — that is a
repeatable experiment. Mishler pointed out that the data matrix is largely
ignored during tree-building: systematists focus on methods of analyzing trees
— Bayesian inference, maximum likelihood, neighbor joining — rather than
checking whether their data were placed correctly into the matrix. That is the
crux of DNA sequence data and models of evolution.

[06:28] THE PROS OF MOLECULAR DATA

Some pros of using molecular — specifically DNA — data in phylogenetic
studies: I can rather quickly discern the genetic causes of a trait I am
interested in. Say I have a group of organisms — bacteria, fungi, or insects —
that is hardy and withstands low temperatures or desiccation. I can compare
closely related sister taxa and discern where that trait lies within a gene. I
can understand critical points of divergence in ancient relationships. I can
take a known regulatory part of a gene and compare it across a wide group of
taxa to see whether they share it. And if I discover a new organism with
sequenced DNA, I can compare it quickly to a large swath of taxa and decide
where it fits on the tree. Mishler stated that scrutinizing DNA sequences is
the workhorse for a systematist, and it underpins every other way we compare
data — whether an RNA transcript, a functional protein, or a gene region —
because it all relies on that DNA molecular data.

[08:05] THE DOWNSIDES OF DNA DATA

Some downsides: a gene located in one area of a chromosome in one organism
could be in a completely different place in another organism, which presents a
problem when comparing sequences — you may miss it in a BLAST search. DNA
tends to evolve in a fairly consistent manner, and if I am looking at an
ancient relationship I would want that change to be "frozen" and not shift
every time I add new data during bootstrapping or reconfiguration. Sequences
that are similar or homologous can be widely conserved — but what if it is an
analogous trait, or homoplasy? Deciding two organisms are related because of
similar sequence data could be an error if convergent evolution is going on.
Finally, from a statistical standpoint, DNA has only four nucleotides to
compare; adding RNA or protein sequences gives many more things to compare,
allowing a wider range of probability statistics. So DNA is limited in that
sense by having only four character states.

[09:46] PITFALLS: HIDDEN CHANGES, CONVERGENCE, AND EXTINCT LINEAGES

Let us look at some pitfalls, to understand why we have nucleotide
substitution models and why we weight our data. In a simple comparison
between two sequences, a single change is easy to see. But if one change is
followed by another at the same site, the intermediate change is lost — I
only see the two end sequences and miss that data. Convergence is another
issue: if a site changes and then changes back, I might conclude two sequences
are homologous when originally there was a separate lineage. Extinct lineages
add difficulty: if a species split off and then went extinct, I might assume A
is more closely related to C, missing the divergent species that is no longer
around to appear in my sequence data. And depending on which extant and
extinct lineages survive, two diagrams can imply very different divergence
times for the same ancestor. Because of issues like these, we need different
models of evolution to provide different algorithms in our computer analyses.
Under Bayesian inference, the Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method — which
you may have seen as options in MEGA and other software — is there to increase
our probability and give better statistical results.

[12:38] WEIGHTING DATA AND THE JUKES–CANTOR MODEL

To do this, we make sure we are weighting our data initially. A good
systematist makes sure the matrix is correct and the data are good to go
before building a tree and comparing it to others. One of the simplest models
is Jukes–Cantor. It states that nucleotide frequencies are weighted equally,
with mutation rate as the parameter. If I take sequence 1 and sequence 2, do a
multiple sequence alignment so the homologous sites line up, and score each
match — since all four nucleotides are weighted equally, each shows up roughly
25% of the time — the match rows sum to 1 and the transition rows sum to 0.
That gives a sense of how these algorithms weight individual characters
(nucleotides). The result is then compared to another sequence, over and over
for all sequences in the alignment, compiling a final score and building your
initial phylogenetic tree.

[14:33] ADDING TRANSVERSIONS: KIMURA'S MODEL

The next substitution model calculates transversions as an additional
parameter, while the transition rate stays the same as in Jukes–Cantor.
Transversions happen when a purine is exchanged for a pyrimidine; this happens
less often than a transition (a purine exchanged for a purine), so it gets a
special score. Through calculus, we come up with a formula that, as a ratio,
eventually produces a gamma distribution with a different type of score for
building your initial tree, which you can then compare against other
phylogenetic hypotheses.

[15:30] ALIGNMENTS AND TREE METHODS

Multiple sequence alignments are the starting point for these analyses. Their
purpose is to take homologous sequences and line them up by their homologous
nucleotide bases; those columns eventually indicate selective pressures and
can inform you of different relationships, functions, or phenotypes. There are
many other models that give different weights to different parameters, or add
parameters. As we were shown, when you add more than two taxa the results
become exponential very quickly — and so does the computing strength needed.
It depends on whether you want a fast computation or have time to wait for a
better educated guess. Here is an example of a multiple sequence alignment
from my phylogeny of interest — a pairwise comparison of 37 taxa, with a
consensus row. Distance-based methods take an alignment and a reference,
convert it into a matrix of scores, and produce a single tree, which is then
compared to other sequences and trees that may already have data attached,
like fossil records. Examples are UPGMA and neighbor joining. Once you lock
that first tree in, the initial data remain fixed. Character-based methods —
maximum parsimony, maximum likelihood, and Bayesian analysis — instead
consider those changes. I would dare to say multiple sequence alignments are
more for a quick analysis to find where you are on the map, or a quick homolog
for an unknown sequence, while character-based methods are for deep,
ancestral-state comparisons.
